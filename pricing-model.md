1. The Strategic Shift
The previous pricing document (Commercial Model v1.0) explored three structural models — Simple Tiers, Modular, and a Hybrid. Model C (the Hybrid) remains the right structural foundation. What has changed is the strategic framing that sits above it.

The fundamental challenge Clarence faces is not pricing resistance — it is inertia. Lawyers are cautious professionals working within adversarial structures that have calcified over decades. We are not asking them to buy software. We are asking them to trust a different way of working.

Pricing is a confidence architecture, not a revenue optimisation exercise. The commercial model must be designed to reduce hesitation at every stage of the customer journey.

2. The Confidence-First Commercial Model
2.1 The Problem With Standard SaaS Pricing
Standard SaaS pricing assumes a buyer who understands the product, recognises the value proposition, and is making a rational procurement decision. Clarence's early customers are not that buyer. They are often sceptical of AI in legal contexts, habituated to adversarial negotiation, and uncertain whether their counterparties would even engage with the platform.

The commercial model must therefore solve for something deeper than affordability. It must solve for credibility, familiarity, and confidence — in that order.

2.2 The Three Confidence Stages
Every Clarence customer moves through three stages before they become an advocate. The commercial model must map to these stages, not fight against them.



The Clarence Academy is the Stage 1 mechanism. It did not exist when the previous pricing document was written. Its introduction materially changes how the funnel works.

3. The Revised Tier Structure
3.1 Reframing the Tiers
The three-tier structure is retained but reframed. The language shifts from capability descriptions to journey descriptions. A visitor should read the tiers and think 'that is where I am' rather than 'that is what I get.'



3.2 The Naming Change: Why 'Explore' Replaces 'Starter'
'Starter' implies the beginning of a commercial relationship. 'Explore' implies agency — the professional is doing the choosing, on their own terms. The distinction matters enormously for an audience of experienced practitioners who would resist any suggestion that they need re-training.

A lawyer who enters via the Explore tier is not a trial user and not a student — they are a professional having a look around on their own terms. Within the Explore tier, they will find the Clarence Academy: a structured programme for those who want to go deeper. But that is their choice to make, not a door they are pushed through. By the time they reach the Platform tier, they have arrived there through curiosity, not coercion.

4. Platform Add-Ons
4.1 The Add-On Philosophy
Add-ons are not upsells. They are the natural next capability for a team that has built confidence in the base platform. The pricing and language should reflect this — each add-on is presented as something the customer discovers they need, not something Clarence is pushing.



The Academy Seat is the renamed and reframed New-Start Seat from v1.0. The change is not cosmetic — it positions the discounted seat as part of the Clarence Academy ecosystem rather than simply a 'cheaper version' of a full seat. Note: the tier is called 'Explore', but the learning product within it is called the 'Clarence Academy' — a deliberate distinction. The tier name welcomes; the product name rewards those who go deeper.

7. Retained Open Questions
The following questions from Commercial Model v1.0 remain open for Data Room discussion. One new question is added.

Annual discounts: 2 months free (~17%) to improve cash flow predictability and reduce churn?
Academy cohort pricing: What is the right per-person price for firm-wide or university Academy licences?
Training standalone: Should the Training Studio be available without the Platform for CPD-only buyers?
Playbook Lite: Should cross-checking be available without live enforcement at a lower price point?
Volume seat pricing: Should per-seat price decrease at volume (seats 4-6 at £49, 7-10 at £39, 11+ at £29)?
Provider pricing: Should respondents remain free at all tiers? This is a competitive advantage worth preserving.
NEW — Enterprise reframing: Should Enterprise be quoted as a transformation programme with a defined scope, rather than a software licence? This changes the buyer (L&D and risk budget vs IT procurement) and the price ceiling.
